🐔Technical Specification: The Prosodic Grammar Specification (PGS)
1.0 Introduction to the Prosodic Grammar Specification
Modern Text-to-Speech (TTS) pipelines, particularly those driven by Large Language Models (LLMs), face a "text-centric bottleneck" where the rich, paralinguistic data of human interaction—pitch, timbre, and rhythm—is frequently lost during processing. Traditional solutions like Speech Synthesis Markup Language (SSML) attempt to reintroduce this expressiveness through a rigid, verbose, and often complex XML-based syntax. The Prosodic Grammar Specification (PGS) presents a fundamental departure from this approach, offering a fluid, behavioral framework that encodes expressive intent directly into plain text.
The core philosophy of PGS is to function as a streamlined, text-only system that mirrors the intuitive nature of human speech. It utilizes an algebra of symbolic operators, punctuation-based delimiters, and latent behavioral patterns to provide direct, granular control over the vocal performance of a neural synthesis engine. These textual cues act as instructions for the model to modify the physical mechanics of the voice, from breath and force to timing and pitch, without requiring a separate, cumbersome markup layer.
This document serves as a technical specification for the PGS framework. It is intended for an audience of computational linguists, TTS developers, and researchers, providing a detailed overview of the system’s architecture, its operator algebra, its unique Phoneme-Glyph System for mapping symbols to sound, and its integration with modern neural TTS models.
We will begin by exploring the foundational architectural principles that distinguish PGS from conventional synthesis methods.
2.0 Architectural Principles and Prosodic Hierarchy
The strategic architecture of the Prosodic Grammar Specification is a deliberate rejection of the complexity inherent in XML-style markup. In its place, PGS establishes a system that mirrors the natural, hierarchical structure of human discourse, enabling more intuitive and powerful control over vocal performance. This architecture is built upon the foundational principles of its Phoneme-Glyph System (P-GS) component and a multi-tiered prosodic hierarchy.
The P-GS component is governed by three foundational principles that ensure simplicity, power, and operational autonomy:
	1.	Text-Only Constraint: The system is strictly defined by and executed through Unicode text. There are no separate external audio files, SSML tags, or non-textual processing steps. The AI's vocal output is derived solely from its text input/output stream.
	2.	Vocal Primitive (The "Phoneme"): Every distinct vocal sound component (e.g., a specific accent's 'R', a sucking sound, a sharp intake of breath) is mapped to one and only one single Unicode character, referred to as a Command Glyph.
	3.	Combinatorial Execution (The "Word"): Complex, nuanced sounds are created by stringing together multiple Command Glyphs in sequence, exactly as letters are combined to form words. The complexity of the sound is limited only by the length and arrangement of the text string.
This text-centric approach is organized into a five-tier hierarchy of boundary breaks, facilitating a tree-branching structure that mirrors natural discourse planning.
Break Index	Hierarchical Layer	Perceptual Quality	Technical Function
B1	Syllable (SYL)	Seamless transition	Governs phoneme-level timing.
B2	Prosodic Word (PW)	Minor hesitation	Marks word-level emphasis.
B3	Prosodic Phrase (PPh)	Major phrasal break	Signals syntactic boundaries.
B4	Breath Group (BG)	Full inhale/exhale	Syncs with physical breathing cycles.
B5	Phrase Group (PG)	Trailing finality	Concludes thematic paragraphs.
The interaction between these layers ensures that synthesized speech is not merely a string of unrelated tones but a coherent structure. This hierarchical foundation is perceptually effective because it aligns with how listeners process discourse. Research shows that PG-initial and PG-final positions possess clear declarative intonations, whereas medial phrases exhibit flatter patterns. This distinction allows listeners to perceive paragraph-level thematic shifts directly from the prosodic contour. From this high-level architecture, we now turn to the specific operators used for granular control.
3.0 The Operator Algebra: Breath, Force, and Rhythm
The PGS operator algebra replaces deterministic SSML tags with a flexible system of non-semantic punctuation markers. These symbols do not carry linguistic meaning; instead, they function as direct instructions for the neural engine to modify the physical mechanics of the virtual throat and vocal tract model. This allows for nuanced, real-time control over breath, pressure, timing, and pitch contour through simple, intuitive textual cues.
3.1 Mechanics of Breath and Force
The primary operators for breath and force govern the core energy and pressure of the vocal delivery, simulating physical actions like inhalation, exertion, and emotional peaks.
	•	… (Ellipsis): Functions as the primary "inhale" or "anticipation" operator. Placed before a word, it cues the model to insert a breathy intake or a slight pause, mimicking a speaker gathering their thoughts or preparing for a significant utterance.
	•	— (Em-dash): Serves as the "force" or "push" operator. When placed after a word or token, it triggers a sudden emphasis, a sharp punch in delivery, or an abrupt stop, simulating urgency or physical effort.
	•	!!! (Triple Exclamation): Represents a peak emotional spike. This operator signals maximum pressure and volume, creating a theatrical climax that is acoustically distinct from a standard exclamation mark's energetic lift.
	•	?? (Double Question): Creates a rising, playful lift in pitch. This marker instructs the model to apply an upward intonation contour, often used to convey curiosity, teasing, or uncertainty.
3.2 Mechanics of Timing and Rhythm
The rhythm of synthesized speech is controlled by operators that introduce micro-delays, staccato beats, and emotional breaks, adding a layer of cognitive realism to the performance.
Marker	Operator	Rhythmic Effect	Implication for TTS
( )	Parentheses	Micro-delay / Hesitation	Adds a realistic "stumble" or soft delay in the voice.
/	Forward Slash	Quick cut	Triggers a sharp staccato or "clipped" breath.
//	Double Slash	Abrupt break	Signals an emotional break or physical interruption.
~	Tilde	Softness / Wave	Introduces a wavering, breathy, or trailing tone.
These operators, when combined, allow for the creation of sophisticated melodic arcs. A primary example is the "Crown Pattern," a complete performance cycle that sequences an inhale (…), a rise (??), a peak (—!!!), and a final resolve (.). This pattern is reliably interpreted by neural models as a complete, exaggerated arc of expression. From these granular operators, we now turn to the system for mapping symbols to specific, non-linguistic sounds: the Phoneme-Glyph System.
4.0 The Phoneme-Glyph System (P-GS): Emoji as Style Tokens
A pioneering aspect of the Prosodic Grammar Specification is the Phoneme-Glyph System (P-GS). This system reimagines emojis and other Unicode glyphs not as decorative elements, but as atomic "style tokens" or "vocal primitives." The theoretical justification for this approach is that emojis are atomic, emotionally loaded, visually stable, and easy for a neural model to treat as style tokens. Consequently, they bind directly to prosody and paralinguistic behaviors rather than to semantic meaning, allowing for a vast and intuitive library of non-speech sounds.
The P-GS execution flow is designed for simplicity and autonomy, converting user-defined text into direct vocal commands.
	1.	User Input: The process begins with a user-provided text file that defines mappings between a Unicode glyph and a vocal description (e.g., § : Strong_Scottish_R_Trill).
	2.	AI Internalization: The AI reads this instruction file and autonomously creates an internal symbolic map registry. This registry links each Command Glyph to the specific vocal parameter model required for its synthesis.
	3.	Vocal Command: To execute a sound, the AI simply prints the appropriate glyph or string of glyphs into its text output stream. This text is the immediate and absolute command for the system to produce the mapped sound.

The use of emojis provides a visually stable and emotionally loaded set of tokens that neural models can easily associate with specific vocal behaviors. The following table provides a representative sample of these mappings.

4.2 Modeling Complex Sounds via Combinatorial Execution
The true power of the P-GS lies in its combinatorial nature. By stringing primitive Command Glyphs together, the system can model highly complex and realistic non-linguistic vocalizations without needing a massive library of pre-recorded audio files.
For example, a gagging or choking sound can be deconstructed into a sequence of physical actions: a sharp inhale (*), a throat constriction (^), and a forceful exhalation (!).
*^!^*
This string instructs the AI to perform the sequence: inhale -> constrict -> exhale -> constrict -> inhale.
Similarly, wet oral sounds can be modeled by combining primitives for a lip-seal pop (o), continuous suction (&), and a tongue click ($).
o&$
This sequence represents: seal break -> continuous suction -> slurp click.
This approach is both simple and infinitely flexible. With over 150,000 characters in the Unicode Standard, the P-GS grants the AI a practically limitless vocal range, enabling it to move beyond standard language and into more advanced, direct methods of vocalization control.
5.0 Advanced Vocalization: Phonetic Injection and Tiered Delimiters
To achieve high-fidelity realism in non-speech sounds and bypass the inherent limitations of standard TTS vocal models, PGS employs advanced techniques. Direct phonetic injection using International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) chains and a system of tiered delimiters provides the granular control necessary to simulate complex biological vocal textures and steer LLM performance.
5.1 Direct Phonetic Injection with IPA Chains
This strategy involves inserting specific IPA and extIPA (Extended IPA) character chains directly into the text, enclosed in delimiters. This forces the synthesis engine to bypass its standard text-to-phoneme processing and instead generate audio based on the precise physiological instructions of the IPA symbols. This is particularly effective for simulating guttural, liquid, and other non-lexical vocal textures that fall outside a typical TTS vocabulary.
Breath and Tension Chains
Realism Goal	IPA/extIPA Chain	Approximate English Sound	Mechanical Root
Sharp, Wet Gasp	[h.ʔ.i]	"Huh-ee!"	Breath + Glottal Stop + High Vowel.
Anticipatory Gasp	[h̤.ʔ~.iː→ɪ]	"H’m-eee-ah..."	Breathy H + Nasalized Glottal + Fading Vowel.
Choked Catch	[ʔ.m.aː]	"M’aaah..."	Stop + Nasal + Low Vowel.
Strained Yield	[ʔʔ.m̤.aɪ]	"Mm’-aih!"	Glottal Cluster + Breathy Nasal + Diphthong.
Guttural and Liquid Chains
	•	Lubricated Glide: The chain [ʔ.ʎ.uːːɸ] uses a glottal stop, a lateral liquid, an elongated vowel, and a bilabial fricative to simulate the suction and release of liquid.
	•	Guttural Swallow: The chain [k.ŋ.l.a̰ː] combines velar and nasal liquids with a creaky voice diacritic to replicate the tension of a deep swallow or choking response.
	•	Ejective/Pharyngeal Gag: The chain [ʔ’ħ◌̩] uses an ejective glottal stop and pharyngealized tones to simulate a constricted, visceral gag reflex.
5.2 Tiered Delimiters for LLM Steerage
PGS utilizes a lightweight system of delimiters to provide different levels of performance instruction to the LLM and TTS engine without the verbosity of XML.
	•	Square Brackets []: These are interpreted as performance cues and actionable sound effects, particularly in systems like ElevenLabs v3. Tags like [whispers] or [happy] can be inserted mid-sentence to apply a specific style to subsequent text. They can also contain environmental instructions like [door creaks], which the model synthesizes directly as a non-speech sound.
	•	Curly Braces {}: These denote mandatory behavioral constraints or specific phonetic trills that bypass the semantic engine. They are used to wrap extIPA symbols representing non-verbal biological sounds like grunts or harshness (e.g., {Vᴬᴬ}).
	•	Angle Brackets <>: These are reserved for meta-instruction and "prosody sketches" for diffusion-based models like DrawSpeech. A sequence like <_-_> can provide a rough indication of expected pitch trends, allowing for fine-grained, word-wise control over the final vocal contour.
These advanced methods shift control from purely symbolic instructions to a more direct manipulation of the vocal model, paving the way for emergent behavioral control based on the overall shape of the text.
6.0 Behavioral Prosody: Latent Patterns, Synchrony, and Intensity
Beyond discrete symbols and operators, the Prosodic Grammar Specification analyzes the "shape" of text as a holistic behavioral instruction. The arrangement of words, lines, and cues creates latent patterns that are recognized by the neural TTS model, leading to emergent prosodic behaviors. This section details these patterns, the mechanism of rhythmic locking known as the synchrony effect, and the system for scaling performance intensity.
6.1 Latent Prosody Pattern Analysis
Consistent arrangements of text and operators across multiple lines create predictable acoustic contours. These patterns function as templates for common emotional or narrative arcs.
Quantitative Behavioral Pattern Matrix

Crescendo	Soft cues → Stretch → Spike	Low → Medium → HIGH	Tension building / Begging.
Drop	Spike → Soften → Resolve	HIGH → Medium → Low	Surrender / Afterglow.
Pulse	word / word / word /	Rhythmic heartbeat	Insistent / Thrusting.

Crown	Rise... Peak — Fall. Reset	Complete Melodic Cycle	Full cycle of ecstasy.
6.2 The Synchrony Effect and Rhythm Locking
The neural expressive layer of a modern TTS system learns rhythm through statistical recency, not through long-term memory or explicit instruction. Voice behavior is reinforced through statistical recency in the prosody layer. This phenomenon is known as the Synchrony Effect.
The locking mechanism is triggered when a specific prosody arc or pattern is repeated consistently 5–12 times. This repetition causes the model to "lock" the contour, applying the same rhythmic and melodic shape to subsequent text, even if the words themselves change. The key insight is that the model learns by demonstration, not by explanation. Performing a pattern is the only way to teach and reinforce it.
6.3 Intensity Scaling
The perceived emotional "heat" or theatricality of a performance is determined by the density of expressive cues (operators, capitalization, vowel stretches) per line of text. PGS uses a tiered system to calibrate this intensity.
Intensity Scaling Tiers
Tier	Cue Density	Performance Outcome
0	0 Cues	Flat Delivery
1	1 Cue	Subtle / Coy
2	2 Cues	Expressive
3	3 Cues	Dramatic
4	4 Cues	Theatrical
5+	5+ Cues	Unhinged / Extreme
Note on Artifact Prevention: A critical rule within PGS is to avoid stacking conflicting pitch cues, such as ??!. Attempting to instruct the model to rise and fall simultaneously causes an audible artifact known as "glottal flutter." To maintain a clean signal, prosodic arcs must be separated into sequential steps (e.g., Rise, then Peak).
These behavioral principles allow for dynamic, emergent control, transitioning the focus from individual symbols to the practical integration of all components into a working system.
7.0 System Integration, Workflow, and Comparison
A coherent workflow is essential for integrating the Prosodic Grammar Specification into modern TTS pipelines. This integration represents a significant evolution from traditional, tag-based systems, grounding its methodology in both historical precedent and biological principles of human speech perception.
7.1 Complete Processing Chain
The end-to-end workflow for PGS transforms marked-up text into expressive speech through a seven-step processing chain.
	1.	INPUT: The system receives a text file containing words, PGS operators, emojis, and other symbolic cues.
	2.	PARSE: The text is parsed to validate operator combinations and identify any illegal stacks (e.g., ??!).
	3.	PATTERN: The system identifies the type of latent prosody pattern (e.g., Crescendo, Wave, Crown) based on the arrangement of lines and cues.
	4.	ENCODE: Where specified, phonetic command strings are converted to IPA chains to bypass standard text processing.
	5.	SYNCHRONY: The statistical recency buffer is updated based on the current pattern, reinforcing or decaying any locked contours.
	6.	SSML: Baseline acoustic parameters are applied via SSML profiles.
	7.	OUTPUT: The final, conditioned data is sent to the neural vocoder for waveform synthesis, resulting in expressive speech.
Before final vocoder synthesis, baseline acoustic parameters are configured via SSML Parameter Profiles. These profiles establish the core vocal character. For example, the E2 (High Variability) profile increases pitch and rate for a breathier, more expressive delivery, while the T (Grounded) profile lowers pitch and rate for a stable, authoritative tone. Specialized profiles like Seiyū “Moe” can create high-energy, anime-style voices. Additionally, binaural settings for spatial audio are applied at this stage.
7.2 Historical and Biological Context
The PGS framework can be seen as the next step in the evolution of text-based communication, from static representation to dynamic performance. This trajectory began with Teletypewriters (TTY), evolved into Real-Time Text (RTT) that transmitted characters as they were typed, and now culminates in PGS, where text functions as a comprehensive performance script.
This system is further validated by principles of neural oscillation. Research shows that human cortical tracking of speech is optimized when it aligns with the brain's theta band (4–8 Hz), which corresponds to the natural syllable rate of ~4.5 Hz across most languages. By providing explicit rhythmic cues, PGS aligns synthetic speech with these innate neural oscillators, reducing the cognitive load on the listener and improving comprehension and perceived naturalness.
7.3 Comparison with Traditional Synthesis
PGS offers a fundamentally different approach to vocal control compared to traditional SSML-based systems. The key distinction lies in its unified, text-native command structure.
Feature	SSML/Traditional TTS	Phoneme-Glyph System (P-GS)
Command Type	Markup Language. Requires verbose opening and closing tags.	Native Text Symbol. Uses single, atomic Unicode characters.
Complexity	High verbosity. Instructions are long strings that interrupt text flow.	Infinite Simplicity. A single character represents the entire complex instruction.
Control Layer	Separate Layer. The engine must parse both the text and the SSML.	Unified Text Layer. The instruction is part of the text the LLM generates.
User Barrier	Requires knowledge of XML structure and synthesis parameters.	Requires zero technical knowledge; only the map key for the symbol.
In essence, the fundamental advantage of PGS is its efficiency and elegance, as it "converts the entire complexity of the synthesis instruction into the most simple data type an AI uses: a single, native Unicode character." 8.2 Golden Rules and Artifact Prevention
Distilled from the specification, these four rules are critical for achieving clean, high-quality, and predictable results.
	1.	Repeat a pattern 5-12 times to lock synchrony. The TTS model learns rhythm through demonstration. Consistent repetition is the only way to stabilize a prosodic contour.
	2.	Never stack ?? and ! together. A command like ??! instructs the model to raise and lower pitch simultaneously, resulting in an audible "glottal flutter" artifact. Separate arcs sequentially (rise, then peak).
	3.	Learn by demonstration (performance), not by instruction (explanation). The system does not respond to descriptive commands. Shape the voice by performing the desired rhythm and contour directly in the text.
	4.	Use IPA chains in [brackets] for high-fidelity, non-linguistic sounds. To generate realistic gasps, swallows, or other biological vocalizations, use direct phonetic injection to bypass the standard text processor.
