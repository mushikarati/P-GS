🐠 Expressive Neural TTS via Text Cues
    
Modern neural TTS can generate very natural speech, but making it expressive (emotional, nuanced, characterful) using only plain text is still challenging.  In practice, most systems only react to basic punctuation and simple cues , so writers have developed text‐based “tricks” to shape prosody.  For example, a community guide for the open-source Tortoise TTS strongly recommends using punctuation and spacing to guide breathing and cadence (commas, ellipses, dashes to force pauses)  .  Repeating letters or phonetic spellings (e.g. “Mmm”, “Aaaah”, “uhhh”) is also advised to simulate drawn‐out or breathy sounds .  One style guide even notes, “if a human reader would pause and let his or her voice drop, we need to indicate this in text” .  Similarly, capitalization or multiple exclamation marks can cue emphasis or excitement  .  In short, textual formatting and punctuation – ellipses, commas, exclamation points, repeated letters, ALL CAPS, etc. – are key tools to hint at intonation, stress, and pauses without any speech markup.
•	Punctuation for pauses and tone: Commas, colons, ellipses and question/exclamation marks are used to break up speech and alter intonation.  For instance, many neural TTS models implicitly treat “?” as a rising pitch cue and add a pause after “.” or “,” .  Writing guides emphasize using extra punctuation wherever a breath or change of cadence is desired  .
•	Letter repetition and elongation: Typing long vowels or consonants (e.g. “soooo” or “rrrrrr”) and onomatopoeic syllables (like “mmmh” or “uhh”) forces the model to stretch sounds or simulate moans and sighs . This is a common hack for making speech sound breathier or more drawn-out.
•	Capitalization/Emphasis: Putting a word in ALL CAPS or adding multiple exclamation points can make the voice emphasize it.  Guides recommend “capitalizing an entire word for stronger emphasis” .  (This mimics stage direction for stress.)
•	Explicit stage/sound cues: Some users insert bracketed cues in the text (even if TTS doesn’t officially support them) to shape delivery.  For example, adding “(whisper)” or “[soft breath sound]” to a script has been reported to produce more whispery or breathy outputs in practice  .  A blog on ASMR voice tactics even suggests including soft-sound descriptions like “[soft breath]” or “[finger tapping]” so the model generates those effects .
    
By combining these tricks (short vs long sentences, careful punctuation, explicit cues), script writers can coax the model into more dynamic rhythm and pacing.  As one guide puts it, a well-placed comma or ellipsis can create a “softer breath” or natural pause  .  Others note that shorter sentences speed up the delivery (for excitement), while longer ones slow it down (for reflection), essentially using text structure to control pacing.

ch Science and Prosody Insights
    
Under the hood, neural TTS learns prosody (stress, intonation, rhythm) mostly from text features.  Classic studies confirm that punctuation is a dominant prosodic cue.  Delmonte & Tripodi (2015) note that “current TTS systems … only take into account information coming from punctuation” and that symbols like commas, question marks and exclamation points directly “modify the prosody” (e.g. rising pitch on a question) .  In data-driven TTS research, punctuation is explicitly used to predict phrasing: for example, classifiers for pitch accent consider “the punctuation following each word” as a feature .  This aligns with the text-based tricks above: adding commas or ellipses will actually train or trigger the model to pause or lower pitch.
    
More advanced speech science reminds us of nuances beyond punctuation.  Prosodic features like pitch accent and boundary tones are influenced by syntax and semantics.  Modern TTS often uses context embeddings to shape intonation and stress.  For example, large TTS models (like Google’s Tacotron-based systems) learn intonation patterns that, say, emphasize certain words or rise at questions, based on statistical correlations in training data .  However, even these powerful models can “ignore strong text cues” unless explicitly guided .  Gao et al.’s BASE-TTS paper (2024) observes that “prosody predictors… often ignore strong text cues that would force a dramatic change (e.g. shouting or whispering)” .  In other words, unless a cue is explicit (like all-caps or an emoji), the model may default to neutral prosody.
    
Some speech research also highlights rhythm and stress: elongated vowels (often written as doubled letters) naturally correlate with drawn-out speech, and italics or punctuation can signal new vs. old information which affects pitch accent.  While academic papers assume carefully annotated data for prosody, in practice we simulate these cues in text.  For instance, voice scientists note that placing words like “new” information tends to get stressed unless marked otherwise .  In TTS prompting, writers similarly use text markers (like vs vs _ vs ALL CAPS) to force stress.
    
LLMs and Style-Driven TTS
    
An emerging approach is to use large language models to enrich text prompts for speech.  LLMs can rewrite or annotate text to better encode prosodic intent.  For example, AcoustAI describes LLM-augmented TTS pipelines where a model like GPT-4 can “rewrite text for clarity and delivery” or even “insert SSML-like markup for tone, emphasis, and pause” .  In practice, one might prompt an LLM with instructions (e.g. “Add emphasis, breathing, or whisper cues to this script”) and feed the LLM’s output to the TTS engine.  This bridges the gap between text and speech prosody without requiring manual markup.  Prompt engineering with LLMs also allows simulating different personas: by crafting style descriptions (“friendly, excited voice” vs. “calm, seductive tone”), the resulting text leads to different prosodic realizations.  In fact, recent work shows that even simple prompts like “male, British accent, low-pitch, fast speaking speed” can produce markedly different speech styles .
    
In academic speech research, this trend appears as “prompt-based TTS”.  For instance, Lee et al. (2025) propose converting persona descriptions into TTS style prompts, enabling fine control of pitch, emotion, and rate via text alone .  These systems treat the style specification as part of the text input (a “voice prompt”) and the acoustic model learns to adjust prosody accordingly.  Large pretrained speech-LMs (like a GPT for speech) also hint at context-driven prosody: by training a Transformer on paired text and audio, models can capture subtle content–prosody links (e.g. changing pitch on repeat utterances) without explicit prosody labels .
    
Takeaways
    
In summary, expressive prosody from plain text relies on textual cues that TTS models respond to.  Key methods include:
ation & Formatting: Deliberate use of commas, periods, ellipses, etc., to force pauses or intonation changes  .
•	Orthographic Tricks: Repeating letters or using ALL CAPS to simulate elongation or emphasis  .
•	Style Words in Prompts: Including descriptive adjectives or bracketed cues (e.g. “[whisper]”, “breathy”) to bias tone  .
•	Prosody-aware TTS Models: Employing models that interpret textual style prompts (as in Octave TTS) which can transform “plot twists, emotional cues” in text into speech variation .
•	ASMR/Erotic Focus: In scenarios like ASMR or erotic narration, adding breath and voice texture cues is crucial – either via text hints (sound descriptors, slowed pacing) or specialized model controls (sliders for breathiness)  .
    
Research continues to expand these techniques.  For example, new TTS architectures use diffusion models conditioned directly on text (eliminating intermediate features) and claim easy adaptation for expressive control .  Others propose letting users draw a pitch/energy curve (“prosody sketch”) as input .  In practice, combining all available methods — careful script writing, prompt engineering with LLMs, and adaptive TTS models — yields the richest prosody from text. The field is actively exploring how to bridge plain text and human-like intonation, promising ever more immersive narrative and character voices in the near future.
